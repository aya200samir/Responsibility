Executive Summary

Current AI governance frameworks are built on a fundamentally flawed premise: that law can treat artificial intelligence outputs as deterministic events subject to binary standards of negligence. They cannot. AI systems are inherently probabilistic — and the legal world has yet to reckon with what that means.

This White Paper presents an original framework: Probabilistic AI Governance (PAG). Rather than asking "was the AI accurate?", PAG asks the more precise and legally actionable question: "Where on the output distribution did this decision fall — and was a human present when it mattered most?"

The framework operationalises this through a novel application of the Normal (Gaussian) Distribution of AI outputs as a legal threshold, defining three governance zones — Safe (Green), Advisory (Amber), and Danger (Red) — each carrying distinct obligations of human oversight and legal liability.

In high-stakes environments, particularly warfare and armed conflict, where AI systems are already influencing targeting, intelligence analysis, and command decisions, this framework argues that existing legal standards of care must be recalibrated from deterministic to probabilistic logic. The consequences of failure are not technical errors. They are potential violations of International Humanitarian Law.

Section I

The Accountability Gap: Why Current AI Law Is Failing

Modern AI legislation — including the European Union's AI Act (2024), the U.S. Executive Order on AI (2023), and the broader declarations emerging from international summits such as Bletchley (2023) — represents genuine progress. These instruments acknowledge risk, mandate transparency, and begin to trace the contours of liability.

Yet they share a common, critical failure: they speak in categories, not in calculus. They classify AI systems by domain (healthcare, defence, judiciary) and attach risk labels accordingly. They mandate "human oversight" in general terms. What they do not do — what no binding legal framework has yet done — is define, with mathematical precision, when that human must intervene.

"The world's AI governance frameworks tell us that the human must be in the loop. None yet tells us precisely at which moment the loop requires them."

This gap is not merely academic. In practice, decision-makers, operators, and developers face a deeply ambiguous standard of care. A military commander who relies on an AI targeting recommendation faces no established threshold defining when reliance constitutes gross negligence. A legal counsel using AI-generated analysis in an active conflict faces no codified duty to scrutinise outputs that fall outside acceptable confidence bounds.

The doctrine of "best efforts" and "reasonable care" — inherited from tort law — cannot be stretched to cover systems that operate not on logic but on learned probability distributions. We need new architecture.

Section II

From Nascent Technology to Decisive Actor: The Aviation Parallel

Every transformative technology begins its legal life in a state of tolerated uncertainty. In the early decades of commercial aviation, liability frameworks were deliberately tempered by the principle known in Islamic jurisprudence as Al-Ghurm bil-Ghunm — broadly translated as "the burden is commensurate with the benefit." If a passenger chose to fly knowing the risks, they bore a portion of those risks. Courts and regulators reflected this by limiting airline liability in the technology's formative years.

This was not moral indifference. It was rational policy: innovation requires space to fail safely, to iterate, to mature. Society accepts higher risk thresholds for nascent technologies because the societal gain from their development justifies the exposure.

⚖
The Al-Ghurm bil-Ghunm Principle in AI Context

Early AI deployment in low-stakes environments — recommendation engines, content curation, logistics optimisation — reasonably attracted reduced liability exposure. The user derived benefit from the system and absorbed proportionate risk. This calculus holds where errors are recoverable and consequences bounded.

AI has crossed that threshold. It is no longer a nascent curiosity operating at the margins of human decision-making. It now operates at its centre — in courtrooms, hospital triage systems, financial markets, and, most critically, in active armed conflict. When an AI system contributes to the identification of a military target, or generates a legal memorandum advising on the permissibility of a strike, it is a decisive actor. The "nascent technology" defence is no longer tenable.

Aviation reached this same inflection point. Once it became mass transportation infrastructure, liability frameworks shifted. Strict liability replaced assumed risk. Regulatory standards became exacting. AI is at its own inflection point now.

Section III

The Statistical Model: Normal Distribution as Legal Threshold

The foundational insight of this framework is deceptively simple: AI hallucination is not a defect to be fixed. It is a mathematical reality to be governed.

Large language models, predictive systems, and generative AI do not reason deterministically. They sample from learned probability distributions. This means that for any given input, an AI system does not produce a singular correct output — it produces an output that is, in essence, a weighted draw from a statistical space. Most draws will cluster around a mean. A small but irreducible proportion will fall in the tails — statistically improbable, potentially erroneous, potentially dangerous.

The Core Statistical Architecture

For each deployment domain — medical diagnosis, legal analysis, military intelligence — the AI system's output distribution can be characterised and studied. Over sufficient observation, this distribution approximates a Normal (Gaussian) Curve. This is not a simplification; it is a measurable, domain-specific empirical reality.

Figure 1 · Gaussian Output Distribution — Legal Threshold Model

The Legal Translation

The transformative step this framework proposes is to treat the statistical properties of this distribution not merely as engineering metrics, but as legally operative thresholds. Where an AI output falls on the distribution determines the legal standard of care required of the human actor.

Governing Principle
Legal Obligation = f ( |Output − μ| / σ , Decision Stakes )
As statistical deviation increases, human oversight obligation escalates proportionally with decision stakes.

This is not metaphor. Domain-specific confidence intervals, calibrated by expert panels and regulatory bodies within each field, establish the quantitative bounds of permissible automation. An output within the confidence interval carries a presumption of permissible reliance. An output in the tails — a statistical outlier — carries a legal presumption of heightened risk, triggering mandatory human review.

Failure to review an outlier output before acting on it is not merely a technical oversight. Under this framework, it constitutes gross negligence per se.

Section IV

Three Governance Zones: A Framework for Tiered Accountability

The Normal Distribution framework yields three distinct governance zones, each with clearly defined rights, obligations, and legal consequences. The confidence thresholds defining each zone are domain-specific — set by a competent regulatory authority or expert body — and scale with the gravity of the decisions at stake.

Zone I — Safe
Green Zone

Output falls within the established confidence interval. Statistical behaviour is in-distribution. Automated action is legally permissible under the Al-Ghurm bil-Ghunm principle — risk has been pre-quantified and accepted.

CI ≥ 95% (standard) · 99%+ (critical)
Zone II — Advisory
Amber Zone

Output falls between 1σ and 2σ from mean. Statistical reliability is reduced. Human oversight is required — a qualified expert must review before any consequential action is taken. Non-review triggers elevated negligence exposure.

Output at 1σ–2σ deviation
Zone III — Danger
Red Zone — Outlier

Output is a statistical outlier beyond 2σ. Potential hallucination or error. Human-in-the-loop is an absolute legal necessity, not a discretionary safeguard. Automated action without review constitutes gross negligence and may attract criminal liability in conflict contexts.

Output beyond 2σ — zero automation
⚑
Risk-Weighted Thresholds: The Stakes Scalar

Confidence thresholds are not uniform. A film recommendation system may operate acceptably at 80% CI. A medical diagnostic tool requires 95%+. A military targeting system in armed conflict demands 99.9% CI with mandatory triple human review for any output classified as an Outlier. The stakes of the decision multiply the governance obligation.

Section V

Liability Mapping: Who Bears Responsibility — and When

Under traditional legal frameworks, liability in AI systems is allocated through post-hoc analysis, often after harm has occurred. This framework proposes a prospective liability map: responsibility is pre-assigned by role, and its weight scales with where on the distribution the acting output fell.

Actor Primary Obligation Liability Trigger Aggravating Factor
Developer Design systems that compute and communicate output confidence. Disclose domain-specific distribution behaviour. Failure to implement confidence reporting. Concealment of known distribution failures. Deployment in high-stakes environments without adequate uncertainty quantification architecture.
Operator Establish domain-specific confidence thresholds. Ensure Amber/Red Zone outputs trigger human review protocols. Absence of zone-based review protocols. Permitting automated action on Outlier outputs. Active suppression of override mechanisms. Incentivising speed over statistical safety.
Decision-maker Treat Outlier-flagged outputs as presumptively suspect. Apply independent professional judgment before acting. Reliance on Outlier output without review, especially where zones are disclosed. Context of armed conflict, where IHL obligations heighten the standard of care.

The Conflict Context Multiplier

International Humanitarian Law — specifically the principles of distinction and proportionality under the Geneva Conventions and their Additional Protocols — imposes affirmative, non-delegable duties on combatants and command structures. These duties cannot be discharged to an algorithm.

Where an AI system is used to support targeting, intelligence assessment, or legality review in armed conflict, and where that system produces an Outlier output that is acted upon without human review, the resulting harm is not a technical error. It is a potential war crime. The liability chain runs from the decision-maker through the operator to, in appropriate cases, the developer — and may extend to command responsibility under international criminal law.

"War does not suspend law — it submits law to its most severe examination. And in that examination, the excuse of algorithmic error will not survive scrutiny."

Section VI

Core Principles of Probabilistic AI Governance

  • 01
    Hallucination as Governance Reality, Not Engineering Failure AI systems are probabilistic by design. Output errors in the statistical tails are mathematically inevitable. Governance must be designed around this reality — not in the expectation that it will be engineered away.
  • 02
    Calibrated Confidence as Legal Instrument The confidence interval of an AI system's output, calculated against its domain-specific distribution, is not merely an engineering metric. It is a legally operative threshold that determines the standard of care owed by each actor in the chain.
  • 03
    Human-in-the-Loop as Triggered Obligation, Not Constant Burden Mandatory human review should not be distributed uniformly across all AI outputs — this is inefficient and unworkable at scale. Review is triggered when outputs enter the Amber or Red Zones, making oversight targeted, expert, and proportionate to actual risk.
  • 04
    Domain-Specific Threshold Setting by Competent Authority Confidence interval thresholds cannot be generic. Each deployment domain requires thresholds calibrated by qualified experts in that field — medical, legal, military — reflecting the actual consequences of erroneous outputs in that context.
  • 05
    Proportional Liability Across the Distribution Chain Legal responsibility flows proportionally: from developer (design obligation), to operator (deployment obligation), to decision-maker (reliance obligation). The weight of each actor's liability scales with the deviation of the output from the distribution mean and the stakes of the decision.
Conclusion

Redesigning Decision-Making for the Probabilistic Era

The central challenge of AI governance is not technical. It is conceptual. Legal systems built on deterministic logic — where a professional either met the standard of care or did not — are ill-equipped to adjudicate failures in systems that speak only in probabilities.

The question is not whether AI is accurate. The question is: at what point on its output distribution was this decision taken, and was a qualified human present to exercise judgment when the mathematics demanded it?

The framework advanced in this paper — the Probabilistic AI Governance model grounded in the Normal Distribution as legal threshold — offers a path forward. It does not seek to stop AI from operating autonomously. It seeks to redesign the architecture of decision-making so that automation and human judgment are not alternatives, but partners governed by statistical law.

In the most dangerous environments human beings have ever created — armed conflicts, life-critical medical decisions, irreversible legal determinations — the need for precision, accountability, and principled governance has never been more urgent.

The probability does not decide. The human does. But only if we design the system to demand it.
